The Problem

5 steps to enter your own home.

Ring → notification → open app → check video → tap unlock. Fine for visitors. Frustrating when you're the one standing at the door with shopping bags.

Research

What I heard from 8 smart lock users.

Informal conversations with friends, family, and online contacts. Not formal research — directional insights.

01

Most people bypass their smart lock

6/8 people use a physical key or PIN more than the app. The app is too slow at the door.

02

Security anxiety is about "letting others in"

People aren't worried about their own entry. The stress is remote-unlocking for someone else.

03

Nobody wants fully automatic unlock

Every person rejected auto-unlock. Even if the tech were perfect, it felt "wrong."

Key Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the concept.

01

Confirmation tap, not auto-unlock

Everyone rejected auto-unlock. The tap preserves agency: you chose to open the door.

→ One step (vs 5), security preserved.

02

On-device processing, no cloud

Face data is GDPR "special category." All matching on-device using embeddings, not photos. No cloud = no breach risk.

→ Privacy-first. Trade-off: household members only.

03

Liveness detection required

Without it, a photo could trigger unlock. Depth sensing + micro-movement adds 1–2 sec delay. Testers read the pause as "the system is being careful."

→ Perceived as thoroughness, not slowness.

Edge Cases

Scenarios that break a naive implementation.

Children under 13

GDPR restricts biometric processing for minors. Face + PIN required. Parents set time-based rules.

Elderly members

Face recognition is opt-in. PIN pad and physical key remain parallel entry methods.

Temporary guests

Time-limited access codes via app. Auto-expire, logged.

Delivery workers

Never auto-unlock for unknown faces. Standard video call flow.

Low light & weather

IR LEDs for night. Below confidence threshold? Falls back to PIN.

Emergency / duress

Silent alarm PIN: opens door normally, silently alerts emergency contacts.

Reflections

Security and convenience aren't opposites

The current 5-step flow isn't more secure. A face match + tap is faster and arguably safer than a quick video glance.

The hardest UX problems are emotional

Everyone understood auto-unlock could work. They still didn't want it. Designing for the feeling of control mattered more.

GDPR as a design tool

On-device processing, explicit consent, instant deletion. Not just legal requirements — trust-building features.

I worked alone — it shows in the research

8 informal conversations isn't enough. A real project needs structured interviews, diverse demographics, physical prototype testing.
