Yale Smart Doorbell

Yale's smart doorbell requires 4–5 steps to let someone in. I explored how facial recognition could reduce that to one — without compromising the security that makes a doorbell matter in the first place.

The Problem

Getting into your own home shouldn't take 5 steps.

Yale's Smart Video Doorbell works with their smart locks through the Yale Home app. When someone rings the bell, you get a notification, open the app, check the video feed, confirm who it is, then tap to unlock. For a delivery person or a guest, that flow makes sense. For you coming home with bags of groceries, it's friction that shouldn't exist.

The doorbell already has a camera pointed at faces all day. The question I wanted to explore: what if the system could recognise household members and offer a faster path in — without removing the security layer that makes smart locks trustworthy?

Research & Discovery

How do people actually get into their homes?

I spoke with 8 people who use smart locks or video doorbells — friends, family, and a few contacts from online communities. These were informal conversations, not structured interviews. I also looked at how competitors (Ring, Nest, Aqara) handle the recognition-to-entry problem.

Insight 01

Most people bypass their own smart lock

6 out of 8 people I spoke with admitted they use a physical key or PIN code more often than the app. The app flow is too slow when you're standing at the door with shopping bags or a child.

Insight 02

Security anxiety is highest for "letting others in"

People weren't worried about their own entry. The stress came from remotely unlocking for a delivery person, cleaner, or family member. "What if I unlock and it's not actually them?"

Insight 03

Nobody trusts fully automatic unlock

When I described a system that auto-unlocks on face recognition, every person hesitated. The idea of a door opening without any human confirmation felt "wrong" — even if the technology were perfect.

Proposed Flow

Two paths: recognised and unrecognised.

The core design idea is simple: if the doorbell knows who you are, it shortcuts the process. If it doesn't, it falls back to the existing secure flow. The system never auto-unlocks — it always requires one deliberate action.

Key Design Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the concept.

Decision 01

Confirmation tap, not auto-unlock

The single biggest decision. Every person I spoke with rejected the idea of a door that opens automatically when it sees your face. Even with 99.9% accuracy, the psychological comfort of a deliberate action matters.

The tap on the doorbell serves two purposes: it's a security confirmation, and it gives the user a sense of control. You chose to open the door. The system didn't decide for you.

→ This keeps the interaction to one step (vs. 5 currently) while preserving the user's sense of agency and security.

Decision 02

On-device face processing, no cloud

Biometric data is classified as "special category" under GDPR. Uploading face data to a cloud server creates legal exposure, breach risk, and user anxiety. The concept proposes all face matching happens on-device, with face embeddings (mathematical representations, not photos) stored locally on the doorbell.

This limits the system to household members only (no "recognise my Deliveroo driver" feature), but that constraint actually simplifies the UX and strengthens the privacy story.

→ Privacy-first architecture that reduces GDPR compliance burden and builds user trust. The trade-off: no cloud-based "smart" features like visitor history with faces.

Decision 03

Liveness detection as a non-negotiable

Face recognition without liveness detection is a security theatre. Someone could hold up a photo of a household member and get the unlock button. The concept requires active liveness checks — depth sensing and micro-movement analysis — before showing the unlock option.

This adds a 1–2 second delay to the recognition step. I considered whether that delay would frustrate users, but in testing the prototype flow, people actually interpreted the brief pause as "the system is being careful" — which increased trust.

→ A small delay that users perceive as thoroughness, not slowness. Security and UX aligned rather than in tension.

Edge Cases & Security

The scenarios that test whether a concept actually works.

A smart doorbell concept is only as good as its worst edge case. I mapped the scenarios that would break a naive implementation and designed fallbacks for each.

👶

Children under 13

GDPR restricts biometric data processing for children. The concept allows children to be enrolled with parental consent, but requires a PIN code alongside face recognition as an additional factor. Parents can also set time-based access rules.

👴

Elderly household members

Not everyone is comfortable with facial recognition. The concept keeps the existing PIN pad and physical key as parallel entry methods. Face recognition is opt-in per person, never forced on the whole household.

🎒

Temporary guests

A friend staying for a week shouldn't need face enrollment. The concept includes time-limited access codes that can be shared via the app. These expire automatically and log every use.

📦

Delivery & service workers

The system never auto-unlocks for unrecognised faces. Delivery and service visits go through the standard video call flow. A future iteration could explore one-time unlock codes sent to verified delivery accounts.

🌙

Low light & weather

Face recognition degrades in poor lighting and rain. The concept uses the doorbell's existing IR LEDs for night recognition, but falls back to PIN entry when confidence is below threshold. No guessing — if it's not sure, it asks for a PIN.

🚨

Emergency & duress

What if someone forces a household member to unlock the door? The concept includes a "silent alarm" PIN — a specific code that opens the door normally but silently alerts emergency contacts. This is a feature that exists in some alarm systems and translates well to smart locks.

GDPR & Biometric Data

Under GDPR Article 9, facial recognition data is "special category" biometric data. The concept addresses this through: explicit opt-in consent per household member, on-device processing (no cloud storage of face data), ability to delete all biometric data instantly from settings, and no third-party access to face embeddings. A real implementation would need a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) and clear legal basis under Article 9(2)(a) — explicit consent.

What I'd Validate Next

This is a concept. These questions need real answers.

I've designed the flows and thought through the edge cases, but a concept without validation is just an opinion. These are the questions I'd need to answer before this could move toward implementation.

?

Would people actually enroll their faces?

Smart home users are a privacy-conscious group. Even with on-device processing, would enough household members opt in to make the feature viable? I'd need to test this with real Yale users, not just people I know.

?

Does the 1-tap flow feel secure enough?

In my prototype walkthroughs, people liked the concept. But testing a flow on a screen is very different from standing at your front door. The real test is whether the single tap on the doorbell feels like enough of a security gesture in context.

?

Can the hardware support on-device face matching?

I designed around on-device processing for privacy, but I don't know if Yale's current doorbell hardware has the processing power for real-time face matching with liveness detection. This constraint could change the entire architecture.

Reflections

What I learned from designing for security.

Security and convenience aren't opposites

The instinct is to think "more secure = more friction." But the current 5-step flow isn't more secure — it just feels like work. Facial recognition with a confirmation tap is both faster and arguably more secure than someone glancing at a video feed and tapping unlock.

The hardest UX problems are emotional, not functional

Everyone I spoke with understood that auto-unlock could work technically. They still didn't want it. The resistance wasn't rational — it was about feeling in control of their home. Designing for that feeling mattered more than optimising the flow.

GDPR isn't a constraint — it's a design tool

Treating biometric data regulations as a design constraint actually led to a better product. On-device processing, explicit consent, instant deletion — these aren't just legal requirements, they're trust-building features that users actively want.

I worked alone on this — and it shows in the research

8 informal conversations aren't enough to validate a concept this consequential. If this were a real project, I'd want structured interviews, a larger sample with diverse demographics, and proper usability testing with physical prototypes at actual front doors.

What I'd Do Differently

Start with Yale's real users, not assumptions

I designed this based on general smart home user conversations. But Yale's user base might have very different attitudes toward biometrics than the tech-savvy people I spoke with. Older homeowners, families with security concerns, and people in different cultural contexts might reject or embrace this very differently.

Prototype the physical interaction earlier

I designed the flow in Figma, but the critical moment — tapping a button on a doorbell while holding shopping bags — is a physical interaction that screens can't simulate. I should have built a rough physical mockup to test the ergonomics of the tap gesture.

Engage with the technical constraints sooner

I designed the ideal UX first and assumed the hardware could support it. In reality, the processing power, camera quality, and IR capabilities of the Yale doorbell would significantly constrain what's possible. A real project would need hardware-software co-design from day one.
